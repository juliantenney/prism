### Worked Example: How Supply and Demand Curves Determine Market Equilibrium

Consider a simple market for apples. The supply curve (S) and demand curve (D) represent the relationship between price and quantity supplied or demanded.

- **Supply Curve (S):** Shows the quantity of apples producers are willing to sell at each price. It slopes upward because higher prices incentivize producers to supply more.
- **Demand Curve (D):** Shows the quantity of apples consumers want to buy at each price. It slopes downward because higher prices discourage consumption.

#### Step 1: Initial Equilibrium  
The market equilibrium is where supply equals demand — the curves intersect. Suppose at price \$2 per apple, quantity supplied and demanded is 100 apples. This is the equilibrium price and quantity.

#### Step 2: Increase in Demand  
Imagine a health report increases demand for apples. The demand curve shifts rightward from D to D1.

- At the original price of \$2, quantity demanded now exceeds quantity supplied, creating a shortage.
- Producers respond by raising prices.
- The new equilibrium is at a higher price, say \$3, and a larger quantity, say 120 apples.

#### Step 3: Decrease in Supply  
Suppose a frost damages apple crops, reducing supply. The supply curve shifts leftward from S to S1.

- At the original price, quantity supplied falls short of quantity demanded, causing a shortage.
- Prices rise to balance the market.
- The new equilibrium price might be \$4, with a reduced quantity of 80 apples.

#### Key Points in Reasoning  
- **Shifts in curves** represent changes in market conditions (e.g., consumer preference, production costs).
- **Movements along curves** represent changes in quantity supplied or demanded due to price changes.
- The intersection point moves accordingly, adjusting equilibrium price and quantity.

This example shows how supply and demand interact dynamically to set market outcomes.

### Sample Analysis: Effects of Supply and Demand Changes on Market Equilibrium

**Scenario:** The government introduces a subsidy for apple farmers, lowering production costs.

**Analysis:**  
- The subsidy effectively increases supply because farmers can produce more at each price.
- The supply curve shifts rightward from S to S2.
- At the original equilibrium price of \$2, quantity supplied now exceeds quantity demanded, creating a surplus.
- Prices will fall to clear the surplus.
- The new equilibrium price might be \$1.50, with a higher quantity of 130 apples.

**Explanation:**  
The subsidy reduces costs, shifting supply right. This lowers prices and increases quantity sold. Demand remains unchanged, so the demand curve is stable. The market moves to a new equilibrium reflecting these changes.

This reasoning links the curve shift to real-world policy effects and market outcomes, demonstrating a coherent understanding beyond definitions.

### Overview of Supply, Demand, and Market Equilibrium

Supply and demand are fundamental economic concepts describing how markets allocate resources.

- **Supply:** The quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to sell at various prices during a certain period. It generally increases with price because higher prices make production more profitable.
- **Demand:** The quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices during a certain period. It generally decreases with price because higher prices reduce consumers’ willingness or ability to buy.

**Market Equilibrium:**  
The point where the quantity supplied equals the quantity demanded. At this price, the market clears with no surplus or shortage.

**Key Principles:**  
- When demand increases (shifts right), equilibrium price and quantity rise.
- When supply increases (shifts right), equilibrium price falls and quantity rises.
- Movements along curves reflect price changes; shifts in curves reflect changes in external factors like income, tastes, or production technology.

Understanding these principles enables analysis of how markets respond to changes and how prices are determined.

### Worked Example: Calculating Opportunity Cost

**Scenario:** You have 3 hours of free time. You can either study economics or work at a part-time job earning \$15 per hour.

**Step 1: Identify Alternatives**  
- Option A: Study economics for 3 hours.  
- Option B: Work for 3 hours and earn income.

**Step 2: Determine Benefits of Each Alternative**  
- Benefit of Option A: Improved understanding of economics (non-monetary but valuable for future career).  
- Benefit of Option B: Earn \$45 (3 hours × \$15/hour).

**Step 3: Calculate Opportunity Cost**  
- If you choose to study (Option A), the opportunity cost is the income foregone by not working: \$45.  
- If you choose to work (Option B), the opportunity cost is the benefit of studying, which is harder to quantify but represents lost learning.

**Step 4: Reasoning**  
Opportunity cost is the value of the next best alternative foregone. Here, choosing one option means sacrificing the benefits of the other.

This example shows how to identify alternatives, quantify benefits, and calculate opportunity cost in a clear, stepwise manner.

### Sample Solution: Opportunity Cost Calculation

**Problem:** You have \$100 and can either buy a textbook or attend a workshop. The textbook improves your knowledge by 20%, while the workshop improves it by 30%. The workshop costs \$80.

**Solution:**  
- Choosing the textbook means spending \$100 and gaining 20% knowledge improvement.  
- Choosing the workshop means spending \$80 and gaining 30% knowledge improvement.  
- Opportunity cost of choosing the workshop: the value of the textbook (\$100 and 20% improvement).  
- Opportunity cost of choosing the textbook: the value of the workshop (\$80 and 30% improvement).

**Explanation:**  
The opportunity cost involves comparing both monetary and non-monetary benefits. The decision depends on which alternative offers greater overall value. This solution links numerical costs with qualitative benefits, showing economic trade-offs.

### Guide to Interpreting Supply and Demand Graphs

Understanding supply and demand graphs is crucial for predicting market changes. Key concepts:

- **Movement Along a Curve:**  
  - Occurs when price changes cause quantity demanded or supplied to change.  
  - Demand curve movement: change in quantity demanded.  
  - Supply curve movement: change in quantity supplied.

- **Shift of a Curve:**  
  - Occurs when factors other than price change (e.g., income, technology).  
  - Demand curve shift: change in demand at every price (rightward = increase, leftward = decrease).  
  - Supply curve shift: change in supply at every price (rightward = increase, leftward = decrease).

- **Interpreting Changes:**  
  1. Identify if the graph shows a movement along or a shift of the curve.  
  2. Determine the cause (price change or external factor).  
  3. Predict the effect on equilibrium price and quantity.

**Example:**  
- A rightward shift in demand increases equilibrium price and quantity.  
- A leftward shift in supply increases price but decreases quantity.

Use annotated graphs to practice distinguishing these changes and their market implications.
