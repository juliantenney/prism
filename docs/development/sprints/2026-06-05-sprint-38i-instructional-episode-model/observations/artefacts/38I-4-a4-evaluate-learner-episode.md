# A4 — Who Gains, Who Loses, and What Should Be Done?

*Learner-facing workbook episode · Evaluate archetype · Inflation anchor*

---

## Where you are in this session

You can now explain what inflation is, measure it with the Consumer Price Index, and analyse how different types of inflation affect households. Those skills answer *what is happening* and *who is exposed*. This activity answers a different question: **given imperfect options, what should be done — and how do you defend that choice?**

Economists do not treat this as a feelings question. When they evaluate inflation outcomes and responses — whether a household budget plan or a policy stance — they work through **competing perspectives**, make **criteria explicit**, weigh **trade-offs**, and produce a **justified judgement** that could change if evidence changes. You will practise that method here, applied to a real household decision under 5% inflation.

---

## Step 1 — Build perspectives before you judge

Inflation never hits everyone at once in the same way. Before recommending any strategy, economists map **who gains, who loses, and who can adapt**.

### Two households, same inflation rate

**Household A — fixed income.**  
Maya earns £2,400 per month after tax. Her salary is reviewed once a year. Rent is £950; groceries and utilities have risen sharply. She saves £150 per month when prices are stable.

**Household B — variable income.**  
James earns about £2,800 per month, including bonuses that have tracked firm performance. His rent is frozen for twelve months. He has more room to switch suppliers and delay non-essential purchases.

At 5% inflation, both face higher prices. But Maya’s **purchasing power** erodes unless something in her budget adjusts. James has more **flexibility** — not immunity, but options.

*Fixed income and variable income do not respond to inflation in the same way. That single difference changes which strategies are realistic.*

### Three lenses economists use

Keep these visible. They will reappear in your criteria.

| Lens | Question economists ask |
|------|-------------------------|
| **Distribution** | Who bears the cost first — savers, borrowers, fixed-wage workers, variable earners? |
| **Adaptation** | Who can renegotiate, switch, or earn more within six months? |
| **Time horizon** | Is this a short spike to ride out, or a sustained squeeze requiring structural change? |

**Pause and write (3–4 sentences):** For Maya’s household, which lens bites hardest in the next six months? Name one group that is *less* exposed than Maya and one reason why.

### Competing perspectives — not just “inflation is bad”

Reasonable people can agree that inflation erodes purchasing power yet **disagree on priority**:

- **Household perspective:** “Protect essentials first; do not let a savings goal block food or heating.”
- **Cautious-saver perspective:** “Keep building reserves — uncertainty may worsen.”
- **Income-seeker perspective:** “Push for a pay adjustment now; a 5% CPI rise is evidence.”
- **Policy perspective (context only):** Central banks may tighten policy to slow inflation, but households cannot wait for macro adjustment. Your job is a **household plan**, not a monetary policy paper.

Good evaluation does not ignore these tensions. It **names** them, then decides which priority dominates **for this case**.

---

## Step 2 — Construct criteria (how economists evaluate choices)

Opinion lists strategies. Evaluation **tests** them. Economists build a small rubric before ranking options. Yours has four dimensions — each tied to the household problem, not abstract virtue.

| Criterion | What you are judging | Why it matters here |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| **Purchasing-power protection** | Does the option slow or offset real income loss? | Maya’s income is fixed; prices are not. |
| **Essential-expense resilience** | Does it protect rent, food, utilities, transport first? | Discretionary cuts alone may not cover grocery and utility spikes. |
| **Feasibility** | Can this household do it within six months without specialist finance skills? | A sound idea you cannot execute is not a plan. |
| **Downside risk** | What fails if inflation rises further or the strategy does not work? | Pause saving vs. run down a thin buffer — different risks. |

**How to use the rubric:** Score 1 (weak) to 3 (strong) *for this household*, not in general. A strategy strong on purchasing-power protection may score poorly on feasibility. That tension is the point.

**Write one sentence:** Which criterion should carry the most weight for Maya in the next six months? Justify with one fact from her situation.

---

## Step 3 — Watch an economist judge (worked example)

**Scenario constants:** Maya, £2,400 fixed monthly income, ~£1,900 essentials, £300 discretionary, £800 emergency fund, £150/month saving habit, 5% inflation with groceries and utilities rising faster than rent.

**Options on the table:**

- **A** — Cut discretionary spending and redirect to essentials  
- **B** — Renegotiate rent or switch utility tariffs and brands  
- **C** — Pause general saving; build a short essentials buffer  
- **D** — Seek a structured pay review citing CPI pressure  
- **E** — Shift savings into inflation-linked or higher-risk assets  

### Weak judgement (what to avoid)

> “Inflation is harmful. Maya should budget better and save more.”

This fails evaluation: no criteria, no trade-offs, no acknowledgement of fixed income. It could apply to any headline.

### Stronger judgement (how economists reason)

> “Maya’s binding constraint is **essential-expense resilience** under **fixed income**. I prioritise **C** partially: redirect £90 of the £150 saving for three months into a food-and-utilities buffer, keeping £60 saving to preserve habit and some cover (**feasibility** moderate, **downside risk** low). In parallel, **D** — a documented pay review request — has moderate feasibility but the highest **purchasing-power protection** if it succeeds; it should start immediately, not after savings run down. **A** is necessary but insufficient alone: £300 discretionary cannot absorb a 6–7% essentials squeeze. **B** is worth attempting (rent freeze unlikely mid-contract, but utilities and brands are actionable). I defer **E**: without specialist advice, **downside risk** and knowledge burden are too high for a thin emergency fund. **Trade-off accepted:** slower long-term saving growth versus lower probability of an essentials shortfall if inflation persists.”

Notice what happened: criteria named, strategies compared, one rejected with evidence, trade-off explicit. The economist did not claim certainty — a pay review may fail. Evaluation is **conditional** and **revisable**.

## Step 4 — Guided judgement (your partial evaluation)

Work on **three** strategies from the list — not all five. Complete the table. Every score needs a **one-sentence justification** citing Maya’s facts (fixed income, £800 buffer, expense mix, etc.).

| Strategy | Purchasing-power (1–3) | Essential resilience (1–3) | Feasibility (1–3) | Downside risk (1=low, 3=high) | Justification |
|----------|------------------------|----------------------------|-------------------|-------------------------------|---------------|
| | | | | | |
| | | | | | |
| | | | | | |

**Prompts if you are stuck:**

1. If you scored **A** high on feasibility, did you also ask whether £300 discretionary is *large enough* when essentials rise faster than 5%?  
2. If **D** scores low on feasibility, what evidence would raise it (e.g. employer policy, union, sector norms)?  
3. Where did two criteria **pull against** each other? Write that tension in the justification column.

**Checkpoint:** Did you reject at least one strategy for a **criterion-based** reason, not because it “sounds bad”?

---

## Step 5 — Independent judgement (your recommendation)

Write **220–280 words** as a decision memo Maya could read. Use this structure:

1. **Priority criterion** — one sentence stating what matters most and why.  
2. **Primary and secondary strategy** — named clearly, in order.  
3. **Trade-off** — what Maya gives up; why that is acceptable.  
4. **Condition for revision** — one piece of evidence that would change your advice.

Do not copy the worked example. Your ranking may differ if you weight criteria differently — but you must **earn** the difference with reasoning.

---

## Step 6 — Verify your evaluation

Mark each **Yes**, **Partial**, or **No**. If two or more are **No**, revise Step 5 before continuing.

| Checkpoint | Response |
|------------|----------|
| I compared at least two strategies using the rubric, not only advocated one | |
| At least two criteria appear by name or clear paraphrase in my memo | |
| I named a trade-off, not only benefits | |
| I explained why my lowest-ranked serious option lost | |
| My advice is specific to fixed-income pressure, not generic inflation advice | |

---

## Step 7 — Reflect on how you evaluated

Answer briefly in your workbook:

1. Which criterion was hardest to apply without bias?  
2. What new evidence would make you reverse your primary strategy?  
3. In one sentence: how is **evaluation** here different from **analysis** of who gains and loses?  
   *Hint: analysis maps effects; evaluation chooses and defends action under trade-offs.*

---

## Step 8 — Transfer to your context

Apply the **same four criteria** to your own budget or a household you know (real or realistic). Write **120–160 words**:

- Which strategy rises in priority in your context?  
- Which drops, and why?  
- One constraint you face that Maya’s scenario did not include.

Evaluation is only complete when judgement crosses from a classroom case to a context you are responsible for.

---

## Closing thought

You have practised how economists move from **description** to **decision**: perspectives first, criteria second, comparison third, defended recommendation last. Inflation policy debates at national level use the same discipline at larger scale — explicit criteria, named trade-offs, revisable conclusions. Your household plan should be equally open to new evidence. When CPI prints next quarter, ask: *which criterion would change, and would my recommendation?*
