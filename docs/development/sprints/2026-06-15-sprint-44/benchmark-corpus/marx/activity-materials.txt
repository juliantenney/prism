## Key Marxist Concepts Explained

**Capitalism**  
Capitalism is an economic system based on private ownership of production (factories, tools, platforms) and the use of wage labour to produce goods for profit. The central aim is accumulation of profit rather than meeting social needs.

**Surplus Value**  
Workers produce value through their labour. However, they are paid less than the value they produce. The difference between what workers produce and what they are paid is called **surplus value**, and it becomes profit for the employer.

**Class Struggle**  
Society is divided into classes with opposing interests: the **bourgeoisie** (owners) and the **proletariat** (workers). Class struggle refers to the ongoing conflict over wages, working conditions, and control.

**Alienation**  
Workers may feel disconnected because they do not control their work, do not own what they produce, and may not see meaning in repetitive labour. This leads to alienation from the product, the process, and themselves.

**Example**  
A delivery worker for an app platform receives a small payment per job. The company earns much more from each delivery. This illustrates surplus value and a class relationship between platform owners and workers.

## Worked Example: Analysing a Workplace Scenario

**Scenario**  
A factory worker produces £200 worth of goods per day but is paid £80 in wages.

### Step-by-step reasoning

**Step 1: Identify the classes**  
The factory owner = bourgeoisie (owns means of production).  
The worker = proletariat (sells labour).

**Step 2: Identify surplus value**  
Value produced (£200) − wages (£80) = £120 surplus value.  
This surplus is kept as profit.

**Step 3: Explain exploitation**  
The worker is exploited because they produce more value than they receive. This difference sustains the capitalist system.

**Step 4: Consider alienation**  
If the worker has no control over production and has no connection to the final product, they may feel alienated.

**Conclusion**  
This scenario illustrates how capitalism depends on wage labour, generates surplus value, and creates structured class relationships.

## Sample Output (Model Paragraph)

In this scenario, the worker represents the proletariat, while the factory owner represents the bourgeoisie. This demonstrates exploitation, as the worker receives only a portion of the value they create. If the worker has little control over their work or connection to the final product, this may also lead to alienation. Together, these concepts illustrate how Marx argued that capitalism systematically benefits owners at the expense of workers.

## Mechanisms of Capitalism

Three core mechanisms explain how capitalism produces inequality and instability:

**Exploitation**  
Workers create value but receive limited wages. This creates a persistent gap between labour and reward.

**Capital Accumulation**  
Profits are reinvested, allowing owners to expand production. Over time, wealth becomes concentrated among fewer individuals.

**Overproduction**  
As firms compete and expand, they may produce more goods than consumers can afford to buy. This leads to unsold stock and falling profits.

### Linking Mechanisms to Outcomes

* Exploitation → inequality (workers earn less than they produce)
* Accumulation → concentration of wealth
* Overproduction → economic crises (recessions, layoffs)

These processes show how outcomes emerge from internal dynamics of the system.

Material: M6 (worked\_example)  
Purpose: worked\_thinking  
Content:

## Worked Analytic Pass: From Mechanism to Outcome

**Scenario**  
A growing firm increases production while keeping wages low.

### Step-by-step analysis

**Step 1: Identify mechanism**  
Low wages = exploitation.

**Step 2: Apply analytical lens**  
Workers have limited purchasing power due to low wages.

**Step 3: Trace consequences**  
Goods are produced in large quantities, but workers cannot afford to buy them.

**Step 4: Identify outcome**  
Unsold goods accumulate → overproduction.

**Step 5: Final link**  
Overproduction reduces profits and can trigger layoffs → economic crisis.

**Conclusion**  
Exploitation contributes to overproduction, showing how mechanisms connect to broader outcomes like instability.

## Case Scenarios

**Case 1: Post-war Europe (1945–1975)**  
Many Western European countries introduced strong welfare states, labour protections, and public services. Workers experienced rising wages, improved working conditions, and increased social mobility. While inequality persisted, it was reduced compared to earlier periods. This suggests capitalism adapted through reform rather than collapse.

**Case 2: Global Financial Crisis (2008)**  
A major financial crisis led to bank failures, unemployment, and recession across multiple economies. Governments intervened to stabilise the system. Inequality increased in the aftermath, and many workers faced unstable employment conditions. This reflects instability within the system.

Activity: Was Marx Right? Final Evaluation

## Criteria for Evaluating Marx

Use these criteria to form your judgement:

**1. Explanatory Power**  
Does Marx’s theory help explain real-world patterns (e.g. inequality, crises)?

**2. Predictive Accuracy**  
Did Marx correctly anticipate how capitalism would develop?

**3. Contemporary Relevance**  
Do his ideas still help make sense of modern issues like digital platforms or global supply chains?

### How to use criteria

A strong evaluation uses all three criteria and recognises both strengths and limitations.
