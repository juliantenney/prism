Activity: Understanding CPI and GDP Deflator Differences  
Activity ID: A1_Understand_CPI_GDP  
Mapped outcomes: Explain the difference between the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the GDP Deflator in terms of basket composition and scope of goods and services measured.

Material: M1_Concept_Exposition_CPI_GDP (text)  
Purpose: concept elucidation  
Content:  
The Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the GDP Deflator are two important measures used to track inflation, but they differ significantly in how they measure price changes over time.

The **Consumer Price Index (CPI)** measures the average change in prices paid by urban consumers for a fixed basket of goods and services. This basket represents typical household consumption patterns and remains constant over time, allowing CPI to reflect how the cost of this specific set of consumer goods changes. Because the basket is fixed, CPI captures price changes for goods and services that households commonly buy, such as food, housing, transportation, and medical care.

In contrast, the **GDP Deflator** measures the change in prices of all domestically produced goods and services in an economy. Unlike CPI, the GDP Deflator uses a **variable basket** that changes each period to reflect the current mix of goods and services produced. This means the GDP Deflator includes prices of investment goods, government services, and exports, as well as consumer goods, but excludes imports. The GDP Deflator is calculated as the ratio of nominal GDP to real GDP, capturing price changes across the entire economy.

Key contrasts include:  
- **Basket composition:** CPI uses a fixed basket of consumer goods; GDP Deflator uses a changing basket of all domestically produced goods and services.  
- **Scope:** CPI focuses on consumer purchases; GDP Deflator covers the whole economy’s output.  
- **Inclusion of imports:** CPI includes imported consumer goods; GDP Deflator excludes imports.  
- **Purpose:** CPI is often used to adjust incomes and benefits for cost of living changes; GDP Deflator is used to convert nominal GDP into real GDP.

Understanding these differences is essential to interpreting inflation data accurately and recognizing which measure is most appropriate for different economic analyses.

Material: M2_Worked_Example_CPI_GDP (worked_example)  
Purpose: worked thinking  
Content:  
**Stepwise Explanation Contrasting CPI and GDP Deflator**

1. **Identify the baskets:**  
   - CPI basket: Fixed set of consumer goods like bread, gasoline, rent, and healthcare.  
   - GDP Deflator basket: All goods and services produced domestically, including consumer goods, machinery, government services, and exports.

2. **Observe basket changes over time:**  
   - CPI basket remains fixed to reflect typical household consumption patterns from a base period.  
   - GDP Deflator basket changes each year to reflect current production quantities and types.

3. **Calculate price changes:**  
   - CPI calculates inflation by comparing the total cost of the fixed basket in the current period to its cost in the base period.  
   - GDP Deflator calculates inflation by comparing nominal GDP (current prices) to real GDP (constant prices).

4. **Example:**  
   Suppose in year 1, the CPI basket costs $100, and in year 2, the same basket costs $110. The CPI inflation rate is:  
   \[
   \frac{110 - 100}{100} \times 100\% = 10\%
   \]

   Meanwhile, if nominal GDP in year 2 is $1,100 billion and real GDP is $1,000 billion, the GDP Deflator inflation rate is:  
   \[
   \frac{1,100 - 1,000}{1,000} \times 100\% = 10\%
   \]

   However, if the production mix changes significantly (e.g., more machinery and less consumer goods), the GDP Deflator inflation rate may differ from CPI.

5. **Interpretation:**  
   - CPI inflation reflects changes in prices affecting consumers directly.  
   - GDP Deflator inflation reflects overall price changes in the economy, including investment and government spending.

This example illustrates how the fixed basket of CPI and the variable basket of GDP Deflator lead to different inflation measures.

Activity: Applying CPI to Calculate Inflation Rate  
Activity ID: A2_Apply_CPI_Inflation_Calculation  
Mapped outcomes: Calculate the inflation rate using price changes from a fixed basket of goods and services as measured by the CPI.

Material: M5_Worked_Example_CPI_Calculation (worked_example)  
Purpose: worked thinking  
Content:  
**Stepwise CPI Inflation Rate Calculation**

1. **Identify basket items, prices, and weights:**  
   Suppose the fixed basket includes:  
   - Bread: Price year 1 = $2, Price year 2 = $2.20, Weight = 30%  
   - Milk: Price year 1 = $3, Price year 2 = $3.30, Weight = 20%  
   - Gasoline: Price year 1 = $4, Price year 2 = $4.80, Weight = 50%

2. **Calculate price relatives for each item:**  
   \[
   \text{Price relative} = \frac{\text{Price in year 2}}{\text{Price in year 1}}
   \]  
   - Bread: \(2.20 / 2 = 1.10\)  
   - Milk: \(3.30 / 3 = 1.10\)  
   - Gasoline: \(4.80 / 4 = 1.20\)

3. **Calculate weighted price relatives:**  
   Multiply each price relative by its weight:  
   - Bread: \(1.10 \times 0.30 = 0.33\)  
   - Milk: \(1.10 \times 0.20 = 0.22\)  
   - Gasoline: \(1.20 \times 0.50 = 0.60\)

4. **Sum weighted price relatives:**  
   \[
   0.33 + 0.22 + 0.60 = 1.15
   \]

5. **Calculate inflation rate:**  
   \[
   \text{Inflation rate} = (1.15 - 1) \times 100\% = 15\%
   \]

This means the overall price level for the fixed basket increased by 15% from year 1 to year 2.

Activity: Analysing Inflation Effects on Household Budgets  
Activity ID: A3_Analyse_Inflation_Household_Impact  
Mapped outcomes: Analyse how inflation affects different household budgets, including fixed-income and variable-income scenarios.

Material: M8_Worked_Analytic_Pass_Household_Inflation (worked_example)  
Purpose: worked analytic pass  
Content:  
**Stepwise Analysis of Inflation Impact on Households**

1. **Household Types:**  
   - Fixed-Income Household: Income remains constant despite inflation (e.g., pensioners).  
   - Variable-Income Household: Income adjusts with inflation (e.g., wage earners with cost-of-living adjustments).

2. **Lenses for Analysis:**  
   - **Distribution:** How inflation redistributes purchasing power between households.  
   - **Adaptation:** How households adjust spending or income to cope with inflation.  
   - **Time Horizon:** Short-term vs long-term effects on household budgets.

3. **Distribution Lens:**  
   - Fixed-income households lose purchasing power as prices rise but income stays the same.  
   - Variable-income households may maintain or increase purchasing power if income rises with inflation.

4. **Adaptation Lens:**  
   - Fixed-income households may cut discretionary spending or seek additional income sources.  
   - Variable-income households might negotiate wage increases or adjust investment strategies.

5. **Time Horizon Lens:**  
   - Short-term: Fixed-income households feel immediate pressure; variable-income households may lag in income adjustment.  
   - Long-term: Variable-income households may fully adjust income; fixed-income households remain vulnerable unless benefits are indexed.

6. **Conclusion:**  
   Inflation impacts households differently depending on income type and their ability to adapt. Fixed-income households are generally more vulnerable to inflation’s negative effects, while variable-income households have more flexibility but may face delays in adjustment.

Activity: Evaluating Household Strategies for Inflation Management  
Activity ID: A4_Evaluate_Household_Inflation_Strategy  
Mapped outcomes: Evaluate strategies households can use to manage the effects of inflation on their finances, defending a justified choice using explicit criteria and trade-offs.

Material: M12_Scenario_Maya_Strategy_Menu (scenario)  
Purpose: Maya household scenario with neutral strategy menu A–E  
Content:  
**Maya Household and Inflation Management Strategies**

Maya’s household faces rising inflation that affects her fixed pension income. To manage this, five neutral strategies are proposed:

- **Strategy A: Budget Tightening**  
  Reduce discretionary spending and prioritize essentials.

- **Strategy B: Income Diversification**  
  Seek part-time work or passive income sources.

- **Strategy C: Inflation-Indexed Investments**  
  Invest savings in assets that tend to keep pace with inflation.

- **Strategy D: Debt Management**  
  Pay down fixed-rate debts to reduce interest burden.

- **Strategy E: Government Benefits Optimization**  
  Apply for inflation-related government support programs.

These strategies are neutral and do not assume any is best. Your task is to evaluate them using explicit criteria.

---

Material: M13_Criteria_Exposition_Evaluate (text)  
Purpose: criteria exposition  
Content:  
**Evaluation Criteria for Household Inflation Strategies**

When evaluating household strategies to manage inflation, consider the following criteria:

1. **Depth of Strategy:**  
   How thoroughly does the strategy address inflation effects? Does it include detailed planning and realistic adjustments?

2. **Adaptability:**  
   Can the strategy adjust to changing inflation rates or unexpected economic conditions? Is it flexible to different scenarios?

3. **Transferability:**  
   Is the strategy applicable to other households or contexts? Can lessons learned be applied beyond the current situation?

Using these criteria helps ensure that evaluations are comprehensive, reasoned, and useful for decision-making.

---

Material: M14_Worked_Judgement_Weak_Strong (modelling_note)  
Purpose: worked judgement contrasting weak and strong strategy evaluations  
Content:  
**Contrasting Weak and Strong Evaluations of Household Strategies**

- **Weak Evaluation Example:**  
  "Strategy A is best because it saves money."  
  This is a slogan-based judgement with no explanation of how or why it works, no criteria applied, and no consideration of trade-offs or adaptability.

- **Strong Evaluation Example:**  
  "Strategy A offers immediate cost savings by prioritizing essential expenses, which is crucial for Maya’s fixed income. However, it may limit quality of life and lacks adaptability if inflation rises sharply. While effective short-term, combining it with Strategy C’s inflation-indexed investments could improve long-term resilience. This shows depth, adaptability, and transferability in evaluation."

**Key features of strong evaluation:**  
- Explicit use of criteria (depth, adaptability, transferability)  
- Justification with reasoning and examples  
- Discussion of trade-offs and limitations  
- Consideration of combining strategies for better outcomes

Use this model to guide your own evaluations.
