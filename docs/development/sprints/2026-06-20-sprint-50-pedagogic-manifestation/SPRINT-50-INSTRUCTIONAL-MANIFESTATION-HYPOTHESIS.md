\# Sprint 50 Instructional Manifestation Hypothesis



\## Status



Working hypothesis.



Derived from:



\* Sprint 49 closure findings

\* Marx learner-page investigation

\* Sprint 50 pedagogic manifestation inventory

\* Activity-level manifestation audit (A1–A5)



This document captures the emerging Sprint 50 understanding before implementation decisions are made.



\---



\# Context



Sprint 49 established that pedagogy is now largely:



\* generated

\* preserved

\* composed

\* rendered



The dominant remaining challenge appears to be learner experience rather than pedagogic generation.



The Marx learner-page investigation demonstrated that PRISM is capable of generating:



\* pedagogic framing

\* cognition guidance

\* worked thinking

\* verification structures

\* reflection prompts

\* transfer opportunities



The pedagogic manifestation inventory further demonstrated that the architecture contains a substantial set of pedagogic and instructional structures.



The challenge is therefore unlikely to be missing pedagogy.



\---



\# Key Observation



PRISM already provides a coherent instructional journey \*\*between activities\*\*.



For example, the Marx learner page progresses through:



1\. Understanding foundational concepts

2\. Understanding explanatory mechanisms

3\. Analysing causal relationships

4\. Evaluating historical claims

5\. Synthesising and transferring learning



This represents a meaningful learning progression rather than a collection of unrelated activities.



However, the same coherence is not always visible \*\*within individual activities\*\*.



\---



\# Revised Problem Statement



The primary learner-experience issue is not missing pedagogy.



The primary learner-experience issue is that instructional sequencing within activities is often implicit rather than explicit.



Activities frequently contain:



\* orientation

\* thinking guidance

\* exposition

\* modelling

\* practice

\* verification

\* reflection

\* transfer



but these components are not always manifested as a clear learner journey.



As a result, learners may encounter rich pedagogic content while still being uncertain about:



\* what to do first

\* what to do next

\* what they are trying to achieve

\* how they know they have succeeded



\---



\# Core Hypothesis



PRISM has largely solved instructional sequencing between activities.



Sprint 50 is focused on manifesting instructional sequencing within activities.



The missing piece is not additional pedagogy.



The missing piece is a learner-visible instructional grammar.



\---



\# Instructional Functions Model



Activities should be evaluated through instructional functions rather than field categories.



Proposed instructional sequence:



\*\*Orient → Think → Study → Do → Check → Reflect → Transfer\*\*



Not every activity must contain every stage.



However, learners should be able to recognise the instructional flow.



\---



\# Instructional Functions



\## Orient



Purpose and relevance.



Learner question:



> Why am I doing this?



Possible sources:



\* activity preamble

\* orienting framing

\* activity framing

\* session framing



\---



\## Think



Disciplinary reasoning guidance.



Learner question:



> How should I approach this intellectually?



Possible sources:



\* reasoning orientation

\* cognition guidance

\* disciplinary thinking prompts



\---



\## Study



Observation, exposition, modelling.



Learner question:



> What does good thinking look like?



Possible sources:



\* exposition

\* worked examples

\* annotated examples

\* sample outputs



\---



\## Do



Practice and application.



Learner question:



> What am I being asked to do?



Possible sources:



\* learner task

\* guided practice

\* analysis tables

\* templates



\---



\## Check



Verification and quality control.



Learner question:



> How do I know I have done this well?



Possible sources:



\* checklists

\* criteria

\* expected outputs



\---



\## Reflect



Metacognition and consolidation.



Learner question:



> What have I learned from this?



Possible sources:



\* self-explanation prompts

\* reflection prompts



\---



\## Transfer



Application beyond the immediate activity.



Learner question:



> Where else does this thinking apply?



Possible sources:



\* transfer prompts

\* application tasks

\* extension activities



\---



\# Classification Rule



A simple placement rule emerges from the investigation.



\## Activity Flow



If the learner needs the information to complete the activity, it belongs within the instructional flow.



Examples:



\* activation prompts

\* exposition

\* worked examples

\* learner tasks

\* expected outputs

\* checklists

\* reflection prompts

\* transfer prompts



\---



\## Companion Guidance



If the information helps the learner frame, interpret, or approach the activity, it belongs within companion guidance.



Examples:



\* reasoning orientation

\* disciplinary thinking guidance

\* common misconceptions

\* strategic hints

\* journey signals

\* why-this-matters framing



\---



\# Companion Guidance Principle



Companion guidance is coaching rather than workflow.



It supports the learner without becoming part of the activity sequence itself.



Learners should be able to engage with the activity without losing the instructional narrative.



Guidance should accompany the activity rather than interrupt it.



\---



\# Emerging Rendering Model



The investigation suggests a promising manifestation model:



| Companion Guidance                       | Activity Flow          |

| ---------------------------------------- | ---------------------- |

| Coaching, framing, disciplinary thinking | Instructional sequence |



Conceptually:



\* guidance accompanies

\* activity progresses



This preserves a coherent learner journey while allowing pedagogic coaching to remain visible and available.



\---



\# Reading Order



The reading-order issues identified during Sprint 50 may be a consequence of missing instructional manifestation rather than a pure rendering problem.



If instructional functions are made explicit:



\*\*Orient → Think → Study → Do → Check → Reflect → Transfer\*\*



then many reading-order decisions become self-evident.



The instructional function determines placement.



The learner follows the learning sequence rather than navigating a collection of pedagogic components.



\---



\# Sprint 50 Success Criterion



A learner should be able to open any activity and immediately answer:



1\. Why am I doing this?

2\. How should I think about it?

3\. What should I do first?

4\. What should I do next?

5\. How do I know I have succeeded?

6\. What should I take away from this?



If those questions can be answered naturally from the rendered activity, the manifestation problem is largely solved.



\---



\# Current Conclusion



The Sprint 50 investigation suggests that PRISM already contains most of the pedagogic structures required to realise the original vision.



The architecture already supports:



\* instructional arcs across activities

\* cognition guidance

\* modelling

\* practice

\* verification

\* reflection

\* transfer



The remaining challenge is to manifest those structures as a coherent instructional experience within each activity.



The emerging hypothesis is therefore:



> PRISM's next major step is not generating more pedagogy. It is expressing existing pedagogy through a clear, recognisable instructional sequence that self-directed learners can follow without inference.
