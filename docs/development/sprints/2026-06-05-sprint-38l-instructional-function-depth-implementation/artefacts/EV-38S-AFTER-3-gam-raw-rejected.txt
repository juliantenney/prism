Activity: Understanding Inflation Measurement Methods  
Activity ID: A1  
Mapped outcomes: Explain inflation measurement methods, Distinguish CPI and GDP Deflator, Identify misconceptions about inflation measures  

Material: M_plan_4 (text)  
Purpose: explanation  
Content:  
Inflation is the general increase in prices of goods and services over time, which reduces the purchasing power of money. Two common methods to measure inflation are the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the GDP Deflator.  

The **Consumer Price Index (CPI)** measures the average change over time in prices paid by urban consumers for a fixed basket of goods and services. This basket reflects typical household consumption patterns and remains fixed during the measurement period, allowing CPI to track how the cost of this specific basket changes. The CPI focuses on consumer goods and services that households typically buy, such as food, housing, transportation, and healthcare.  

In contrast, the **GDP Deflator** measures the change in prices of all domestically produced goods and services in an economy. Unlike CPI, it uses a changing basket based on current production rather than a fixed basket. This means the GDP Deflator captures inflation across the entire economy, including investment goods, government services, and exports, reflecting the prices of goods and services produced rather than just consumed.  

Because of these differences, CPI and GDP Deflator often show different inflation rates. CPI is more focused on consumer experience, while GDP Deflator reflects broader economic price changes. Understanding these distinctions helps clarify how inflation impacts households and the economy differently.  

Material: M_plan_5_2 (worked_example)  
Purpose: worked thinking  
Content:  
Let's explore how CPI and GDP Deflator calculations differ through a stepwise example.  

1. **Step 1: Define the baskets**  
   - For CPI, select a fixed basket of consumer goods and services (e.g., 10 apples, 5 gallons of gas, 2 movie tickets). The quantities remain constant over time.  
   - For GDP Deflator, consider all goods and services produced domestically, which may change in quantity and composition each year.  

2. **Step 2: Collect prices for the base year and current year**  
   - Suppose in the base year, apples cost $1 each, gas $3 per gallon, and movie tickets $10 each.  
   - In the current year, apples cost $1.20, gas $3.50, and movie tickets $11.  

3. **Step 3: Calculate CPI**  
   - Calculate the total cost of the fixed basket in the base year: \(10 \times 1 + 5 \times 3 + 2 \times 10 = 10 + 15 + 20 = 45\) dollars.  
   - Calculate the total cost of the same basket in the current year: \(10 \times 1.20 + 5 \times 3.50 + 2 \times 11 = 12 + 17.5 + 22 = 51.5\) dollars.  
   - CPI inflation rate = \(\frac{51.5 - 45}{45} \times 100 = 14.4\%\).  

4. **Step 4: Calculate GDP Deflator**  
   - Assume the economy produced 100 units of product A and 50 units of product B in the base year, with prices $5 and $10 respectively.  
   - In the current year, production changes to 120 units of A and 40 units of B, with prices $6 and $11 respectively.  
   - Nominal GDP current year = \(120 \times 6 + 40 \times 11 = 720 + 440 = 1160\).  
   - Real GDP current year (using base year prices) = \(120 \times 5 + 40 \times 10 = 600 + 400 = 1000\).  
   - GDP Deflator = \(\frac{1160}{1000} \times 100 = 116\), so inflation rate = 16%.  

This example shows CPI uses a fixed basket and focuses on consumer prices, while GDP Deflator uses a changing basket reflecting current production and captures broader price changes.  

Material: M_plan_6_3 (modelling_note)  
Purpose: misconception challenge  
Content:  
A common misconception is that CPI and GDP Deflator measure the same inflation. While both are inflation measures, they differ fundamentally in scope and basket composition.  

CPI uses a **fixed basket** of consumer goods and services, reflecting what households typically buy. This means CPI captures changes in the cost of living for consumers but ignores changes in production or investment goods.  

GDP Deflator uses a **changing basket** that includes all domestically produced goods and services, including consumer goods, investment goods, government services, and exports. This means it reflects the overall price changes in the economy, not just consumer prices.  

Because of these differences, CPI may show higher inflation if consumer goods prices rise faster than other goods, or lower inflation if consumer prices rise slower. GDP Deflator can be influenced by changes in production patterns and prices of goods not consumed by households.  

Understanding this distinction helps avoid confusing these two measures as interchangeable and clarifies their different uses in economic analysis.  

Material: M_plan_7_4 (misconception_confrontation)  
Purpose: misconception confrontation  
Content:  
Many learners confuse CPI and GDP Deflator as measuring the same inflation rate. This confusion arises because both indexes track price changes over time but differ in their baskets and focus.  

To reconcile this confusion, consider this: CPI is designed to measure the **cost of living** for consumers by tracking a fixed basket of goods and services they buy. It answers the question, "How much more does a typical household pay today compared to the past?"  

The GDP Deflator, however, measures the **price level of all goods and services produced domestically**, including those not purchased by consumers, such as machinery or government services. It answers, "How much have prices changed for everything produced in the economy?"  

By recognizing that CPI focuses on consumer experience and GDP Deflator reflects broader economic price changes, learners can understand why these indexes differ and why both are important for different economic analyses.

Activity: Applying CPI Inflation Rate Calculation  
Activity ID: A2  
Mapped outcomes: Calculate CPI inflation rate, Apply weighted averages, Reflect on inflation calculation relevance  

Material: M_plan_4 (text)  
Purpose: criteria exposition  
Content:  
Calculating the Consumer Price Index (CPI) inflation rate involves several key criteria. First, the CPI uses a **fixed basket** of goods and services representing typical household consumption. This basket is weighted to reflect the relative importance of each item in household spending.  

To calculate CPI inflation:  
- Determine the quantities of each item in the fixed basket.  
- Collect prices for each item in the base year and current year.  
- Calculate the total cost of the basket in both years by multiplying quantities by prices and summing.  
- Compute the inflation rate as the percentage change in the basket cost from the base year to the current year.  

This weighted average approach ensures that items with greater household spending influence the CPI more. The fixed basket concept means the CPI tracks how much more or less it costs to buy the same goods and services over time, reflecting changes in purchasing power.  

Understanding these criteria is essential for accurate CPI inflation calculations and interpreting what the CPI inflation rate means for households.  

Material: M_plan_5_2 (worked_example)  
Purpose: worked thinking  
Content:  
Let's walk through a stepwise calculation of the CPI inflation rate using weighted averages and a fixed basket.  

1. **Step 1: Define the fixed basket and weights**  
   Suppose the basket contains:  
   - Bread: 20 units, weight 30%  
   - Milk: 10 units, weight 20%  
   - Electricity: 5 units, weight 50%  

2. **Step 2: Record prices in base and current years**  
   - Bread: Base year $2/unit, Current year $2.20/unit  
   - Milk: Base year $3/unit, Current year $3.30/unit  
   - Electricity: Base year $10/unit, Current year $11/unit  

3. **Step 3: Calculate basket cost in base year**  
   - Bread: \(20 \times 2 = 40\)  
   - Milk: \(10 \times 3 = 30\)  
   - Electricity: \(5 \times 10 = 50\)  
   - Total base year cost = \(40 + 30 + 50 = 120\)  

4. **Step 4: Calculate basket cost in current year**  
   - Bread: \(20 \times 2.20 = 44\)  
   - Milk: \(10 \times 3.30 = 33\)  
   - Electricity: \(5 \times 11 = 55\)  
   - Total current year cost = \(44 + 33 + 55 = 132\)  

5. **Step 5: Calculate CPI inflation rate**  
   \[
   \text{Inflation rate} = \frac{132 - 120}{120} \times 100 = 10\%
   \]  

This stepwise approach shows how weighted averages and fixed quantities combine to calculate CPI inflation, reflecting how much more it costs to buy the same basket today.

Activity: Analysing Inflation Impact on Household Budgets  
Activity ID: A3  
Mapped outcomes: Analyse inflation impact on households, Apply analytical lenses, Reflect on household budgeting under inflation  

Material: M_plan_4 (text)  
Purpose: criteria exposition  
Content:  
Inflation affects household budgets differently depending on income type, spending patterns, and time horizons. To analyse these impacts, we use several analytical lenses:  

- **Distribution lens:** Examines how inflation affects households with different income types, such as fixed-income versus variable-income households. Fixed-income households receive constant income that does not adjust with inflation, making them more vulnerable to reduced purchasing power. Variable-income households may see income adjustments that help maintain purchasing power.  

- **Adaptation lens:** Considers how households adjust their spending and saving behaviors in response to inflation. This includes changing consumption patterns, seeking discounts, or delaying purchases.  

- **Time-horizon lens:** Focuses on the short-term versus long-term effects of inflation on household budgets. Short-term inflation spikes may cause immediate hardship, while long-term inflation trends may lead to strategic adjustments in income sources or investments.  

Understanding these lenses helps households and analysts evaluate the real impact of inflation on budgets and plan appropriate responses.  

Material: M_plan_5_2 (text)  
Purpose: explanation  
Content:  
To analyse inflation's impact on a household, consider the household's income type and spending flexibility. A fixed-income household, such as retirees relying on pensions, faces a steady income that does not rise with inflation. This means inflation erodes their purchasing power, reducing the quantity and quality of goods and services they can afford.  

In contrast, a variable-income household, such as wage earners with cost-of-living adjustments, may maintain or increase income with inflation, cushioning the impact on their budget. However, inflation can still affect discretionary spending and savings.  

By applying these concepts, we can better understand how inflation affects different households and why some are more vulnerable than others.

Material: A4-scenario (scenario)  
Purpose: scenario  
Content:  
**Maya Household Inflation Management Scenario**  

Maya is a retired teacher living on a fixed pension income of $30,000 per year. Inflation has been rising steadily at 5% annually, but her pension income remains fixed. She faces increasing costs for essentials such as food, healthcare, and utilities. Maya is considering several strategies to manage the impact of inflation on her household budget.  

**Strategy A:** Maintain current spending patterns and rely on savings to cover increased costs.  
**Strategy B:** Reduce discretionary spending significantly, focusing only on essentials.  
**Strategy C:** Seek part-time work or freelance opportunities to supplement income.  
**Strategy D:** Downsize housing to reduce fixed expenses like rent or mortgage.  
**Strategy E:** Invest in inflation-protected securities or assets to grow income over time.  

Maya evaluates these strategies from two perspectives:  
- **Financial security perspective:** Prioritizing stable income and minimizing risk.  
- **Quality of life perspective:** Balancing cost savings with maintaining lifestyle and wellbeing.  

Your task is to evaluate these strategies using operational criteria and make a reasoned judgement about their effectiveness for Maya’s household.  

Material: M_plan_4 (text)  
Purpose: perspective construction  
Content:  
When evaluating household inflation management strategies, it is important to consider different perspectives on gains and losses. Two key perspectives are:  

- **Financial security perspective:** Focuses on maintaining stable income and minimizing financial risk. Strategies that preserve income stability or provide reliable supplemental income score highly here.  

- **Quality of life perspective:** Emphasizes maintaining lifestyle, wellbeing, and discretionary spending. Strategies that reduce financial stress without severely compromising lifestyle are preferred.  

Evaluating strategies requires balancing these perspectives, as some strategies may improve financial security but reduce quality of life, and vice versa. Understanding these perspectives helps frame the evaluation criteria and supports a nuanced judgement of strategy effectiveness.  

Material: M_plan_5_2 (text)  
Purpose: criteria exposition  
Content:  
To evaluate household inflation management strategies effectively, apply these operational criteria dimensions:  

1. **Depth of Strategy:** How thoroughly does the strategy address inflation impacts? Does it include detailed planning, scenario analysis, and contingency measures?  

2. **Feasibility and Adaptability:** Is the strategy realistic for the household’s circumstances? Can it be adapted to changing inflation rates or economic conditions?  

3. **Balance of Perspectives:** Does the strategy balance financial security and quality of life considerations? Does it mitigate risks while preserving wellbeing?  

Applying these criteria helps assess whether a strategy is comprehensive, practical, and sensitive to household needs, enabling a reasoned judgement about its effectiveness.
