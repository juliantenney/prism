## Understanding Inflation and the Consumer Price Index (CPI)  

Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises, leading to a decrease in the purchasing power of money. When inflation occurs, each unit of currency buys fewer goods and services than before, which affects consumers, businesses, and the overall economy. Inflation can result from various economic factors, including increased demand for products, rising production costs, or expectations of future price increases.

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) is a key economic indicator used to measure inflation. It tracks the average change over time in the prices paid by consumers for a basket of goods and services. This basket represents typical consumer purchases such as food, housing, clothing, transportation, and medical care. The CPI is calculated by comparing the current cost of this basket to the cost during a base period, producing an index number that reflects price changes.

CPI plays a crucial role in economic policy and personal finance. Governments and central banks use CPI data to adjust interest rates, set inflation targets, and index social benefits like pensions. For households, understanding CPI helps in assessing how inflation affects their cost of living and purchasing power. By monitoring CPI, individuals and policymakers can make informed decisions to manage inflation’s impact on the economy and personal budgets.

## Household Budget Scenario: The Johnson Family  

The Johnson family consists of two adults and two children living in a suburban area. Their monthly income is $5,000 after taxes. Their typical monthly expenses before inflation include:  
- Housing (rent/mortgage): $1,500  
- Food and groceries: $800  
- Transportation: $400  
- Utilities (electricity, water, internet): $300  
- Healthcare and insurance: $400  
- Entertainment and leisure: $300  
- Savings and miscellaneous: $300  

Over the past year, inflation has caused prices to rise by approximately 6% across most categories. For example, food prices have increased sharply due to supply chain disruptions, while housing costs have risen moderately. The Johnsons have noticed their expenses increasing, but their income has remained the same.

This scenario presents the Johnson family’s budget before and after inflation, highlighting the challenges they face in maintaining their standard of living.

Activity: Evaluating Household Strategies to Manage Inflation  
Activity ID: A3  
Mapped outcomes: Evaluate strategies households can use to manage financial challenges during periods of inflation.

Material: M6 (task_cards)  
Purpose: practice-oriented evaluation of household financial strategies  
Content:  

**Task Card 1: Budget Adjustment**  
- Review and reduce discretionary spending such as entertainment and dining out.  
- Prioritize essential expenses and look for cheaper alternatives.  
- Pros: Immediate savings, easy to implement.  
- Cons: May reduce quality of life and leisure activities.

**Task Card 2: Income Diversification**  
- Seek additional sources of income such as part-time jobs or freelance work.  
- Pros: Increases total household income, offsets inflation impact.  
- Cons: Requires time and effort, may increase stress or reduce leisure time.

**Task Card 3: Bulk Buying and Stockpiling**  
- Purchase non-perishable goods in bulk to avoid frequent price increases.  
- Pros: Saves money on unit costs, reduces shopping frequency.  
- Cons: Requires upfront cash, storage space, risk of waste if not used timely.

**Task Card 4: Energy and Utility Savings**  
- Implement energy-saving measures to reduce utility bills (e.g., LED bulbs, thermostat adjustments).  
- Pros: Lowers recurring expenses, environmentally friendly.  
- Cons: Initial investment may be needed, savings accumulate over time.
