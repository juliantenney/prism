### Material: M_plan_4 (text)

*Purpose:* explanation

Inflation is the general increase in prices of goods and services over time, which reduces the purchasing power of money. To measure inflation, economists use several methods, two of the most common being the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the GDP Deflator.  

The **Consumer Price Index (CPI)** measures the average change over time in prices paid by urban consumers for a fixed basket of goods and services. This basket reflects typical household consumption patterns and remains fixed to capture how the cost of living changes for consumers. The CPI focuses on goods and services that households typically buy, such as food, clothing, housing, and transportation.  

In contrast, the **GDP Deflator** measures the change in prices of all domestically produced goods and services in an economy. It is calculated as the ratio of nominal GDP (current prices) to real GDP (constant prices). Unlike the CPI, the GDP Deflator uses a changing basket of goods and services, reflecting the current production mix in the economy. This means the GDP Deflator captures price changes for a broader range of goods, including investment goods and government services, not just consumer goods.  

Understanding these differences is crucial because CPI and GDP Deflator measure inflation from different perspectives: CPI focuses on consumer expenses with a fixed basket, while GDP Deflator reflects overall economic production with a flexible basket. This distinction affects how inflation is interpreted and applied in economic analysis and policy.

### Material: M_plan_5_2 (worked_example)

*Purpose:* worked thinking

Let's illustrate how the CPI and GDP Deflator calculations differ step-by-step with a simple example.  

**Step 1: Define the baskets**  
- CPI uses a fixed basket of goods and services purchased by consumers, say 10 units of food and 5 units of clothing.  
- GDP Deflator uses the current production quantities, which may change year to year.  

**Step 2: Calculate prices for base and current year**  
- Assume in the base year, food costs $2 per unit and clothing $5 per unit.  
- In the current year, food costs $3 per unit and clothing $6 per unit.  

**Step 3: Calculate CPI**  
- Base year basket cost: (10 × $2) + (5 × $5) = $20 + $25 = $45  
- Current year basket cost: (10 × $3) + (5 × $6) = $30 + $30 = $60  
- CPI inflation rate = \(\frac{60 - 45}{45} \times 100 = 33.3\%\)  

**Step 4: Calculate GDP Deflator**  
- Base year production: 10 units food, 5 units clothing (same as CPI basket for simplicity)  
- Current year production changes to 8 units food, 7 units clothing  
- Base year prices: food $2, clothing $5  
- Current year prices: food $3, clothing $6  
- Nominal GDP current year = (8 × $3) + (7 × $6) = $24 + $42 = $66  
- Real GDP current year = (8 × $2) + (7 × $5) = $16 + $35 = $51  
- GDP Deflator = \(\frac{66}{51} \times 100 = 129.4\)  
- Inflation rate by GDP Deflator = 29.4%  

**Step 5: Compare results**  
- CPI inflation is 33.3%, reflecting fixed basket prices rising.  
- GDP Deflator inflation is 29.4%, reflecting changing production quantities and prices.  

This example shows how the fixed basket in CPI versus the changing basket in GDP Deflator leads to different inflation rates.

### Material: M_plan_6_3 (modelling_note)

*Purpose:* misconception challenge

A common misconception is that the CPI and GDP Deflator measure the same inflation rate. However, they differ fundamentally in scope and basket composition.  

The CPI uses a fixed basket of consumer goods and services, focusing on what households typically buy. This fixed basket means CPI captures how the cost of living changes for consumers over time, holding quantities constant.  

The GDP Deflator, however, uses a changing basket that reflects the current year's production of all domestically produced goods and services, including investment and government spending. This means the GDP Deflator captures price changes across the entire economy, not just consumer goods, and adjusts for shifts in what is produced.  

Because of these differences, the CPI can show higher inflation if prices rise for consumer goods, even if production shifts to cheaper goods. Conversely, the GDP Deflator may show lower inflation if consumers substitute goods or if production shifts to less expensive items.  

Understanding this distinction helps avoid confusion and clarifies why these measures serve different analytical purposes.

### Material: M_plan_7_4 (misconception_confrontation)

*Purpose:* misconception confrontation

Let's confront the confusion that CPI and GDP Deflator measure the same inflation. This confusion arises because both indices relate to price changes, but they do so from different angles.  

The CPI is like a fixed shopping list: it tracks how much more or less it costs to buy the same items over time. This approach highlights the direct impact of price changes on consumers' everyday expenses.  

The GDP Deflator is more like a snapshot of the entire economy's production prices, which can change as the mix of goods and services produced shifts. For example, if consumers buy fewer expensive cars and more affordable electronics, the GDP Deflator reflects this changing composition, while the CPI does not.  

Recognising this difference helps reconcile why CPI inflation often appears higher than GDP Deflator inflation during periods of rapid consumer price increases. It also explains why policymakers and analysts choose one measure over the other depending on their focus—consumer cost of living or overall economic price level.

### Material: M_plan_4 (text)

*Purpose:* criteria exposition

Calculating the CPI inflation rate involves understanding key criteria: the fixed basket concept, weighted averages, and price comparisons over time.  

The CPI uses a fixed basket of goods and services representing typical consumer purchases. Each item in the basket has a weight reflecting its relative importance or share in total consumer spending. These weights ensure that price changes in more significant items affect the overall index more than less important items.  

To calculate the inflation rate, you first determine the total cost of the fixed basket in the base period and the current period. Prices for each item are multiplied by their fixed quantities and weights. The inflation rate is then calculated as the percentage change in the total basket cost from the base period to the current period.  

This method captures how much more or less it costs for consumers to buy the same basket of goods and services over time, reflecting changes in the cost of living. Understanding the weighted average and fixed basket principles is essential for accurate CPI inflation calculations.

### Material: M_plan_5_2 (worked_example)

*Purpose:* worked thinking

Let's walk through a detailed example of calculating the CPI inflation rate step-by-step.  

**Step 1: Define the fixed basket and weights**  
Suppose the basket includes:  
- Food: quantity 10 units, weight 0.6  
- Clothing: quantity 5 units, weight 0.4  

**Step 2: Record prices in base and current year**  
- Base year prices: food $2/unit, clothing $5/unit  
- Current year prices: food $3/unit, clothing $6/unit  

**Step 3: Calculate basket cost in base year**  
- Food cost: 10 × $2 = $20  
- Clothing cost: 5 × $5 = $25  
- Total base year cost = $20 + $25 = $45  

**Step 4: Calculate basket cost in current year**  
- Food cost: 10 × $3 = $30  
- Clothing cost: 5 × $6 = $30  
- Total current year cost = $30 + $30 = $60  

**Step 5: Calculate CPI inflation rate**  
\[
\text{Inflation rate} = \frac{\text{Current basket cost} - \text{Base basket cost}}{\text{Base basket cost}} \times 100 = \frac{60 - 45}{45} \times 100 = 33.3\%
\]

This means prices for the fixed basket have increased by 33.3% from the base year to the current year.

### Material: M_plan_4 (text)

*Purpose:* criteria exposition

Inflation affects household budgets differently depending on income type, spending patterns, and time horizons. To analyse these impacts, we use several analytical lenses:  

- **Distributional Lens**: Examines how inflation affects households with fixed incomes versus variable incomes. Fixed-income households, such as retirees on pensions, face greater challenges as their income does not adjust with rising prices, reducing their purchasing power. Variable-income households may see wages or revenues rise with inflation, potentially maintaining or increasing purchasing power.  

- **Adaptation Lens**: Looks at how households adjust spending, saving, and borrowing in response to inflation. Some may cut discretionary spending or seek inflation-protected investments. Others may struggle to adapt, especially if income is fixed.  

- **Time-Horizon Lens**: Considers short-term versus long-term effects. Inflation may cause immediate budget strain but could also influence long-term financial planning, such as retirement savings or debt management.  

Understanding these lenses helps households and analysts evaluate inflation's varied impacts and develop strategies to manage them effectively.

### Material: M_plan_5_2 (text)

*Purpose:* explanation

To analyse inflation's impact on household budgets, we apply core concepts from economic theory and practical budgeting rules.  

First, purchasing power is key: inflation reduces the amount of goods and services money can buy. For fixed-income households, this means their income buys less over time, leading to budget shortfalls unless adjustments occur. Variable-income households may have income that partially or fully adjusts with inflation, mitigating impact.  

Second, the composition of household spending matters. Households spending a large share on essentials like food and energy may feel inflation more acutely than those with more discretionary spending.  

Third, timing is important. Inflation expectations influence spending and saving behavior. Households anticipating higher inflation may accelerate purchases or seek inflation-indexed income sources.  

By applying these principles, we can systematically analyse how inflation pressures affect different households and identify potential coping strategies.

### Material: M_plan_6_3 (worked_example)

*Purpose:* worked thinking

Consider a fixed-income household with a monthly income of $2,000, spending $1,200 on essentials (food, utilities, rent) and $800 on discretionary items.  

**Step 1: Assess inflation impact on essentials**  
Assume inflation causes a 10% price increase in essentials. The household now needs $1,320 to maintain the same consumption level.  

**Step 2: Income adjustment**  
Since income is fixed, the household still receives $2,000, but essentials cost $120 more, reducing discretionary spending capacity.  

**Step 3: Adaptation**  
The household may cut discretionary spending from $800 to $680 to cover essentials, reducing quality of life or postponing non-essential purchases.  

**Step 4: Time horizon**  
If inflation persists, savings may be depleted, or debt may increase to cover shortfalls, causing long-term financial stress.  

This stepwise analysis shows how inflation erodes purchasing power and forces budget adjustments in fixed-income households.

### Material: M_plan_4 (text)

*Purpose:* perspective construction

Evaluating household inflation management strategies requires understanding the gains and losses from different perspectives.  

From a **Financial Security Focus**, gains include preserving savings, avoiding debt, and ensuring long-term stability. Losses may involve reduced discretionary spending and lifestyle compromises.  

From a **Quality of Life Focus**, gains include maintaining lifestyle and discretionary spending, but losses may include increased financial risk, debt accumulation, or depletion of savings.  

Effective evaluation balances these perspectives, considering Maya’s fixed income and inflation pressures. Strategies that preserve purchasing power and financial security without unduly sacrificing quality of life tend to be more sustainable.  

This perspective construction helps frame how to assess each strategy’s benefits and drawbacks in Maya’s situation.

### Material: M_plan_5_2 (text)

*Purpose:* criteria exposition

To evaluate household inflation management strategies effectively, apply these operational criteria dimensions:  

1. **Depth of Strategy:** How thoroughly has the strategy been planned and worked through? Does it include detailed adjustments, scenario considerations, and contingencies?

Applying these criteria ensures a comprehensive and balanced evaluation of Maya’s strategies.
