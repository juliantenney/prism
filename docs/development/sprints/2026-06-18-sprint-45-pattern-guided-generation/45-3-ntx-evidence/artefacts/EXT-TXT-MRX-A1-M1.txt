Capitalism is an economic system where most production is owned by private firms that employ workers for wages. Marx argues that this arrangement creates a structured relationship between classes: those who own productive assets and those who sell labor.

Surplus value explains how profit is produced. During a working day, workers create value greater than the wages they receive. The difference between created value and paid wage is retained by owners as surplus, which is then reinvested to expand production.

Class struggle follows from this arrangement because workers and owners have different material interests. Owners benefit from increasing productivity and controlling labor costs, while workers seek better wages, working conditions, and security. Over time, these tensions shape social institutions, politics, and public debate.

Alienation describes how workers can become separated from the products they make, from control over their work process, and from a sense of shared purpose with others. In modern workplaces this can appear as repetitive tasks, low autonomy, and weak connection between effort and recognition.
