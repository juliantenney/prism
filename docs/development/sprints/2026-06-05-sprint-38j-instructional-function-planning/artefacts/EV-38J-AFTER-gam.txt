Activity: Understand Inflation Measurement Methods and Differences  
Activity ID: A1  
Mapped outcomes: Explain the difference between the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the GDP Deflator in terms of basket composition and scope of goods and services measured., Describe how inflation impacts household budgets, including effects on real income, savings, and borrowing costs.

Material: A1-M1 (text)  
Purpose: explanatory teaching  
Content:  
Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises, which reduces the purchasing power of money. To measure inflation, economists use several methods, two of the most important being the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the GDP Deflator.  

The **Consumer Price Index (CPI)** measures inflation by tracking the price changes of a fixed basket of goods and services that households typically purchase. This basket remains constant over time, allowing comparison of price changes for the same set of items. Because the basket is fixed, CPI reflects how prices have changed for consumers based on their usual spending patterns.  

In contrast, the **GDP Deflator** measures inflation by considering the prices of all goods and services produced domestically. Unlike CPI, the GDP Deflator uses a variable basket that changes over time to reflect current production and consumption patterns. This means it adjusts for shifts in what is produced and consumed, providing a broader measure of inflation across the entire economy, including investment and government spending, not just consumer goods.  

The key difference lies in **basket composition and scope**: CPI uses a fixed basket focused on consumer goods and services, while the GDP Deflator uses a variable basket covering all domestically produced goods and services.  

Inflation impacts household budgets in several ways:  
- **Real income** decreases because as prices rise, the same amount of money buys fewer goods and services.  
- **Savings** lose value in real terms if interest rates do not keep up with inflation, eroding purchasing power.  
- **Borrowing costs** may increase if lenders raise interest rates to compensate for inflation, making loans more expensive.  

Understanding these differences helps households and policymakers interpret inflation data correctly and manage economic decisions effectively.

---

Material: A1-M2 (worked_example)  
Purpose: worked reasoning  
Content:  
Let's explore how CPI and GDP Deflator measure inflation differently through a stepwise example:

1. **Basket Composition**  
   - CPI: Imagine a household's fixed basket includes bread, milk, and electricity. Prices for these items are tracked over time. For example, if bread costs $2 last year and $2.20 this year, the CPI will reflect this price increase for bread within the fixed basket.  
   - GDP Deflator: Instead of a fixed basket, the GDP Deflator considers all goods produced domestically. If consumers buy less bread and more electronics this year, the basket changes to reflect that shift. Price changes for all these goods influence the GDP Deflator.

2. **Scope of Goods and Services**  
   - CPI focuses only on consumer goods and services purchased by households. It excludes investment goods and government services.  
   - GDP Deflator includes all goods and services produced domestically, such as machinery, government services, and exports.

3. **Common Misconceptions**  
   - Some think CPI and GDP Deflator measure inflation the same way. However, CPI's fixed basket means it may not reflect changes in consumption patterns, while GDP Deflator adapts the basket to current production.  
   - Another misconception is that both cover the same goods. CPI excludes investment and government spending, which GDP Deflator includes.

By understanding these differences, you can better interpret inflation reports and their implications.

Activity: Apply CPI Inflation Rate Calculation  
Activity ID: A2  
Mapped outcomes: Calculate the inflation rate using price changes from a fixed basket of goods and services as measured by the CPI.

Material: A2-M1 (worked_example)  
Purpose: stepped think-aloud calculation  
Content:  
Let's work through calculating the CPI inflation rate step-by-step using a fixed basket of goods:

1. **Identify Basket Items and Prices**  
   Suppose the basket contains three items: bread, milk, and eggs. Their prices in the base period and current period are:  
   - Bread: Base price = $2.00, Current price = $2.20  
   - Milk: Base price = $1.50, Current price = $1.65  
   - Eggs: Base price = $3.00, Current price = $3.30  

2. **Calculate Price Changes**  
   For each item, calculate the price change as a percentage:  
   - Bread: \(\frac{2.20 - 2.00}{2.00} \times 100 = 10\%\)  
   - Milk: \(\frac{1.65 - 1.50}{1.50} \times 100 = 10\%\)  
   - Eggs: \(\frac{3.30 - 3.00}{3.00} \times 100 = 10\%\)  

3. **Apply Weights to Price Changes**  
   Each item has a weight representing its importance in the basket:  
   - Bread: 40%  
   - Milk: 30%  
   - Eggs: 30%  

   Multiply each price change by its weight:  
   - Bread: 10% × 0.40 = 4.0%  
   - Milk: 10% × 0.30 = 3.0%  
   - Eggs: 10% × 0.30 = 3.0%  

4. **Calculate Overall CPI Inflation Rate**  
   Sum the weighted price changes:  
   \(4.0\% + 3.0\% + 3.0\% = 10.0\%\)  

The CPI inflation rate is 10.0%, indicating that overall prices in the fixed basket have increased by 10% from the base period to the current period.

Activity: Evaluate Inflation Management Strategies Using Policy Communication Criteria  
Activity ID: A4  
Mapped outcomes: Summarize the role of policy communication by central banks and governments in managing inflation expectations and influencing household and business economic decisions.

Material: A4-M1 (text)  
Purpose: criteria exposition  
Content:  
Effective inflation management strategies rely heavily on clear and credible policy communication by central banks and governments. When evaluating such strategies, consider the following key criteria:

1. **Clarity of Communication**  
   How clearly does the policy message explain inflation targets, policy intentions, and expected outcomes? Clear communication reduces uncertainty and helps households and businesses make informed decisions.

2. **Credibility and Consistency**  
   Does the policy communication maintain trust by being consistent over time and backed by credible actions? Credibility influences how seriously economic agents take inflation forecasts and policy signals.

3. **Impact on Inflation Expectations**  
   How effectively does the communication shape public expectations about future inflation? Managing expectations is crucial because they influence wage demands, price-setting, and spending behavior, affecting actual inflation.

4. **Economic Stability Support**  
   Does the communication contribute to overall economic stability by guiding households and businesses to adjust spending, saving, and investment in ways that moderate inflationary pressures?

These criteria provide a framework to assess the strengths and weaknesses of different inflation management communication strategies.

---

Material: A4-M2 (scenario)  
Purpose: competing perspectives  
Content:  
Two policy communication strategies are presented below:

**Scenario 1: Central Bank Transparent Approach**  
The central bank issues detailed monthly reports explaining inflation trends, policy decisions, and future outlooks. It holds regular press conferences and engages with media to clarify its inflation target of 2%. This approach emphasizes transparency and aims to build public trust and anchor inflation expectations firmly.

**Scenario 2: Government Reassurance Campaign**  
The government launches a public campaign reassuring households and businesses that inflation will be controlled through fiscal measures. Communication is less frequent and focuses on positive economic growth messages, avoiding detailed inflation forecasts. The approach seeks to maintain confidence without overwhelming the public with technical information.

Each strategy impacts households and businesses differently in terms of understanding, trust, and economic decision-making.

---

Material: A4-M3 (modelling_note)  
Purpose: worked judgement contrast  
Content:  
When evaluating these two strategies, consider the following contrasting judgements:

- **Weak Judgement (Scenario 2)**: The government’s reassurance campaign may appear comforting but lacks detailed information, which can lead to uncertainty among households and businesses. Without clear guidance, inflation expectations may become unanchored, increasing the risk of wage-price spirals and economic instability. The lack of transparency may reduce credibility and limit the strategy’s effectiveness in managing inflation expectations.

- **Strong Judgement (Scenario 1)**: The central bank’s transparent approach provides clear, consistent, and credible communication. By regularly updating the public and explaining policy rationale, it helps anchor inflation expectations, enabling households and businesses to make informed decisions. This transparency supports economic stability by reducing uncertainty and aligning expectations with policy goals.

In summary, effective policy communication requires clarity, credibility, and a focus on shaping expectations to stabilize inflation and guide economic behavior.
