60 min session

Overview

This self-study session explores a deceptively simple but conceptually demanding question: Was Marx right?

The challenge lies in deciding what it means to be ‘right’. Marx offered explanations (how capitalism works), predictions (what it would become), and analytical tools (how to interpret society). These different claims require different types of judgement.

Across this session, you will:

  • Build core conceptual clarity
  • Apply Marx’s ideas to concrete examples
  • Compare predictions with modern evidence
  • Develop and justify your own evaluative judgement

The sequence moves from understanding → application → analysis → evaluation → transfer. By the end, your task is not to reach a fixed answer, but to construct a defensible argument supported by criteria and evidence.

Learning purpose

This session develops your ability to interpret and evaluate a major body of political economy rather than simply recall its content.

You will practise:

  • Using conceptual frameworks to analyse real-world cases
  • Distinguishing between explanation and prediction
  • Applying evaluation criteria to competing claims
  • Constructing justified arguments rather than unsupported opinions

A key difficulty is balancing evidence and interpretation: strong responses explain how ideas work and where they do or do not apply.

Learning outcomes

  • Explain key elements of Marx’s political economy, including historical materialism, mode of production, and class struggle
  • Describe how capitalism is characterised in Marx’s theory through exploitation, alienation, and crisis tendencies
  • Summarise Marx’s major predictions about capitalism
  • Evaluate how far modern capitalism aligns with Marx’s claims
  • Distinguish between Marx as a predictive theorist and as an analytical framework

LO1 — Core Concepts of Marx’s Political Economy

Duration: 10 mins

Step 1 of 5

Read and model

Concept exposition

Key Concepts in Marx’s Political Economy

Historical materialism explains how societies change over time. It argues that economic conditions — especially how people produce goods and organise work — shape social structures, politics, and ideas. Rather than ideas driving change, material conditions set the direction of history.

Mode of production refers to how a society organises production. This includes the tools, technology, and labour relationships involved. For example, feudalism relied on land ownership and serf labour, while capitalism is based on wage labour and private ownership. Each mode of production creates different social relations and conflicts.

Class struggle emerges from these production arrangements. Under capitalism, society is divided mainly between the bourgeoisie (owners of capital) and the proletariat (workers). Their interests conflict: one seeks to maximise profit, the other seeks fair wages and conditions. This tension drives social and economic change.

These concepts are connected: the mode of production shapes class relationships, and class struggle drives historical change.

Example: In a factory system, workers sell labour for wages while owners control production and profits. This creates tension over wages, working hours, and conditions — a clear example of class struggle shaped by capitalism.

Worked example: Explaining class struggle

Model task: Explain class struggle in a short paragraph.

Step 1: Identify the groups involved In capitalism, the key classes are capitalists and workers.

Step 2: Identify their interests Capitalists want profit; workers want higher wages and better conditions.

Step 3: Identify the conflict These goals conflict because higher wages reduce profit.

Step 4: Link to structure This conflict is not personal — it arises from how capitalism organises work and ownership.

Step 5: Draft explanation Class struggle refers to the conflict between capitalists and workers, which arises because each group has opposing economic interests within capitalism.

What experts notice

How experts think

  • The explanation links groups → interests → structural conflict, not just naming classes.
  • The reasoning shows that conflict is systemic, not individual disagreement.

Bridge: Now use the same steps to explain historical materialism or mode of production.

Duration: 12 mins

Modelling note

Moving from definition to analysis requires explaining the mechanism linking concept to example. For exploitation: surplus value extraction explains how profit arises.

Concept exposition

Marx’s Key Features of Capitalism

Marx argued that capitalism is structured around interconnected dynamics. Exploitation involves surplus value extraction. Alienation disconnects workers from labour and its meaning. Crisis tendencies reflect instability due to competition and overproduction. These processes interact: exploitation drives profit, alienation shapes experience, and crises reveal instability.
