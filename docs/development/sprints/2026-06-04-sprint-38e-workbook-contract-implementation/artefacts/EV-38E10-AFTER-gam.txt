### Worked Example: Calculating Inflation Rate Using CPI Data

**Step 1: Understand the CPI values**  
Suppose the Consumer Price Index (CPI) was 240 in Year 1 and increased to 252 in Year 2. The CPI measures the average price level of a basket of goods and services in the economy.

**Step 2: Calculate the inflation rate**  
The inflation rate is the percentage change in CPI from one year to the next. Use the formula:

\[
\text{Inflation Rate} = \frac{\text{CPI in Year 2} - \text{CPI in Year 1}}{\text{CPI in Year 1}} \times 100
\]

Substitute the values:

\[
\frac{252 - 240}{240} \times 100 = \frac{12}{240} \times 100 = 5\%
\]

So, the inflation rate from Year 1 to Year 2 is 5%.

**Step 3: Interpret the result**  
A 5% inflation rate means that, on average, prices for goods and services increased by 5% over the year. This affects the purchasing power of money — consumers need more money to buy the same goods as before.

### Understanding Inflation and the Consumer Price Index (CPI)

Inflation is the general increase in prices of goods and services over time, which leads to a decrease in the purchasing power of money. When inflation occurs, each unit of currency buys fewer goods and services than before.

The Consumer Price Index (CPI) is a key economic indicator used to measure inflation. It tracks the price changes of a fixed basket of goods and services commonly purchased by households. The CPI is calculated by comparing the cost of this basket in the current period to the cost in a base period.

CPI is important because it provides a standardized way to quantify inflation, helping governments, businesses, and consumers understand how prices are changing. It influences decisions such as wage negotiations, pension adjustments, and monetary policy.

By monitoring CPI changes, we can distinguish between nominal price changes (which may be due to individual product factors) and broad inflation affecting the entire economy.

Purpose: Think-aloud expert reasoning on selecting and evaluating inflation management strategies  
Content:  
### Modelling Note: Expert Reasoning on Inflation Management Strategies

When evaluating strategies to manage inflation’s effects on household finances, consider the following expert reasoning steps:

- **Effectiveness:** Does the strategy directly address inflation risks, such as by increasing income, reducing exposure to rising prices, or protecting savings? For example, investing in assets that typically outpace inflation can preserve wealth.

- **Feasibility:** Assess whether the household has the resources and knowledge to implement the strategy. For instance, refinancing a mortgage may reduce fixed costs but requires good credit and timing.

- **Risks:** Every strategy carries potential downsides. For example, investing in stocks may offer inflation protection but exposes households to market volatility.

- **Suitability:** Different households have different needs. Retirees may prioritize preserving capital, while working families might focus on budgeting and income growth.

- **Trade-offs:** Some strategies may improve inflation resilience but reduce liquidity or increase complexity. Balancing these trade-offs is crucial.

By systematically applying these criteria, households can make informed decisions tailored to their circumstances.
