\### Historical Materialism as a Method



Historical materialism is Marx’s way of analysing societies by starting from how people produce their material means of life. Rather than treating ideas, politics, or culture as independent forces, Marx argues that these are shaped by underlying economic structures and social relations. This does \*\*not\*\* mean that economics mechanically determines everything else. Instead, it means that material conditions set limits and pressures within which politics, law, and culture develop.



Applied to capitalism, historical materialism asks questions such as: Who owns the means of production? Who must sell their labour to survive? How do these relationships shape political power, institutions, and social conflict? Capitalism is therefore understood as a \*\*historically specific system\*\*, not a natural or timeless one. It emerged under particular conditions and may change or be replaced under others.



A key implication is that capitalism should be analysed dynamically. Wages, profits, class relations, and state policies are not fixed; they evolve as productive forces, technology, and social struggles change. Historical materialism provides a framework for linking economic organisation to broader patterns of inequality, power, and social change.



\*\*Example:\*\*  

Rather than explaining modern labour law as simply the result of moral progress, a historical materialist analysis would link it to industrialisation, worker organisation, and the need to stabilise capitalist production.

\### Worked Example: Applying Historical Materialism to Capitalism



\*\*Step 1: Identify material conditions\*\*  

In industrial capitalism, production is organised in factories using machinery owned by a small group of capitalists. Most people do not own productive assets and must work for wages.



\*\*Step 2: Identify class relations\*\*  

These material conditions create two main classes: capitalists (owners of capital) and workers (sellers of labour power). Their interests differ: capitalists seek profit, while workers seek wages and security.



\*\*Step 3: Link to social and political outcomes\*\*  

Because capitalists control investment and employment, they have structural power in society. This influences state policies, labour laws, and political priorities. Workers respond through trade unions, political movements, and demands for reform.



\*\*Step 4: Draw an analytical conclusion\*\*  

Capitalism is not just a market system but a social order shaped by class relations rooted in material production.



\*\*Bridge:\*\*  

Now apply the same four-step method to capitalism today. Do not copy the example; use it as a model for structuring your own explanation.

\### Surplus Value, Profit, and Exploitation



Marx’s labour theory of value claims that the value of goods comes from human labour. Workers sell their \*\*labour power\*\* for a wage, but during the working day they typically produce more value than the value of their wages. The difference between what workers are paid and the value they create is called \*\*surplus value\*\*.



Surplus value is the source of profit in capitalism. Importantly, Marx does not define exploitation as cheating or immoral behaviour by individual employers. Exploitation is \*\*structural\*\*: it arises because workers must sell their labour to survive, while capitalists own the means of production and appropriate surplus value.



This explains how profit can exist even when wages appear fair or contracts are voluntary. The issue is not exchange in the market, but the organisation of production itself.



\*\*Example:\*\*  

If a worker produces goods worth £200 in a day but is paid £100 in wages, the remaining £100 is surplus value captured by the firm.

Material: A2-WE-1 (worked\\\_example)  

Purpose: worked\\\_thinking  

Content:



\### Worked Example: Identifying Surplus Value



\*\*Step 1: Labour input\*\*  

A worker spends a full day producing commodities.



\*\*Step 2: Wage payment\*\*  

The worker is paid a wage sufficient to cover basic living costs.



\*\*Step 3: Value produced\*\*  

During the working day, the worker produces value exceeding the wage paid.



\*\*Step 4: Surplus identified\*\*  

The excess value is surplus value, which becomes profit once goods are sold.

Material: A3-WE-1 (worked\\\_example)  

Purpose: worked\\\_thinking  

Content:



\### Worked Analysis: From Accumulation to Crisis



\*\*Step 1: Accumulation\*\*  

Firms reinvest profits to expand production and reduce costs.



\*\*Step 2: Competition\*\*  

Firms compete by introducing new technologies and lowering prices.



\*\*Step 3: Concentration of capital\*\*  

Larger firms outcompete smaller ones, leading to fewer, more powerful firms.



\*\*Step 4: Crisis tendency\*\*  

Overproduction, falling profits, or financial instability can emerge from these dynamics.



\*\*Bridge:\*\*  

Apply this causal chain to each scenario without assuming crisis is inevitable.

\### Scenario A: Competitive Manufacturing Sector



A national manufacturing industry consists of many small firms competing on price. Firms continually reinvest profits to automate production, reducing labour costs but increasing output.



\### Scenario B: Highly Concentrated Industry



A small number of multinational corporations dominate an industry. Entry barriers are high, profits are large, and investment increasingly flows into financial assets rather than production.
