Activity: Chronological Analysis of Karl Marx's Life Phases  
Activity ID: A1  
Mapped outcomes: Analyze the key phases of Karl Marx's life and their influence on his philosophical and political ideas.

Material: M1 (text)  
Purpose: Unordered list of key events in Karl Marx's life  
Content:  
- 1883: Karl Marx dies in London after years of political activism and writing.  
- 1848: Publication of *The Communist Manifesto*, co-authored with Friedrich Engels.  
- 1818: Karl Marx is born on May 5th in Trier, Prussia (now Germany).  
- 1843: Marx marries Jenny von Westphalen, his lifelong partner and supporter.  
- 1849: Marx moves to London after political exile from Germany and France.  
- 1835–1841: Studies law and philosophy at the universities of Bonn and Berlin.  
- 1859: Publication of *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy*, marking the beginning of his economic writings.  
- 1867: First volume of *Das Kapital* is published, presenting a detailed critique of capitalism.  
- 1842: Begins work as a journalist in Cologne, writing for radical newspapers.  
- 1871: Marx writes *The Civil War in France*, analyzing the Paris Commune.  
- 1844: Writes the *Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts*, exploring alienation and human nature.  
- 1824–1835: Childhood and early education in Trier, influenced by Enlightenment ideas and the political climate of the time.

Material: M3 (text)  
Purpose: Summary of relevant historical events and Marx's theories  
Content:

**Key 19th-Century Historical Events:**  
- **Industrial Revolution:** Rapid industrialization transformed economies, creating a new working class subjected to harsh labor conditions and exploitation.  
- **1848 Revolutions:** A series of political upheavals across Europe demanding democratic reforms and social change, highlighting class tensions.  
- **Paris Commune (1871):** A radical socialist government briefly took control of Paris, demonstrating working-class self-governance and conflict with state power.  
- **Economic Crises:** Periodic capitalist crises caused widespread unemployment and social unrest, revealing systemic contradictions.  
- **Political Repression:** Governments often censored radical ideas and persecuted activists, influencing Marx’s emphasis on revolutionary action.

**Marxist Theoretical Developments:**  
- **Class Struggle:** History is driven by conflict between social classes, particularly between the bourgeoisie and proletariat.  
- **Alienation:** Workers become estranged from their labor, the products they create, and their own human potential under capitalism.  
- **Commodity Fetishism:** Social relationships are masked by the exchange of commodities, obscuring exploitation.  
- **Historical Materialism:** Material economic conditions shape social structures and ideas.  
- **Capitalist Crises:** Capitalism contains internal contradictions leading to periodic economic breakdowns.  
- **Revolutionary Praxis:** Theory must be linked to practical action to overthrow capitalism and establish socialism.

Activity: Comparative Analysis of The Communist Manifesto and Das Kapital  
Activity ID: A3  
Mapped outcomes: Compare and contrast the main themes and arguments in Marx's major works, such as The Communist Manifesto and Das Kapital.

Material: M4 (text)  
Purpose: Paired extracts from The Communist Manifesto and Das Kapital  
Content:

**The Communist Manifesto (1848) – Extract:**  
*"The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles... The proletarians have nothing to lose but their chains. They have a world to win."*  
This passage captures the revolutionary call to the working class to rise against capitalist oppression and seize political power.

**Das Kapital (Volume 1, 1867) – Extract:**  
*"The commodity appears at first sight an extremely obvious, trivial thing. But its analysis brings out that it is a very strange thing, abounding in metaphysical subtleties and theological niceties."*  
Here Marx introduces the concept of commodity fetishism, beginning a detailed critique of capitalism’s economic structures and social relations.

Activity: Applying Core Marxist Concepts to Contemporary Issues  
Activity ID: A4  
Mapped outcomes: Apply core Marxist concepts to contemporary social, economic, and political issues.

Material: M6 (text)  
Purpose: Summary of core Marxist concepts  
Content:

- **Class Struggle:** The ongoing conflict between different social classes, primarily between those who own the means of production (bourgeoisie) and those who sell their labor (proletariat). This struggle drives social change.

- **Alienation:** Under capitalism, workers become disconnected from the products they create, the labor process, their own human potential, and other people, leading to a sense of powerlessness and loss of identity.

- **Commodity Fetishism:** Social relationships between people are expressed as relationships between commodities, obscuring the exploitation inherent in capitalist production.

- **Historical Materialism:** The idea that material economic conditions and modes of production fundamentally shape society’s structure, culture, and politics over time.

Purpose: Brief overview of contemporary social, economic, or political issues  
Content:

**Contemporary Issues Suitable for Marxist Analysis:**

- **Income Inequality:** The widening gap between rich and poor, with wealth concentrated in the hands of a few, while many struggle with low wages and precarious employment.

- **Labor Rights:** Challenges faced by workers including job insecurity, exploitation, and the decline of unions in the face of global capitalism.

- **Globalization:** The expansion of global markets and multinational corporations, affecting local economies, labor standards, and cultural identities.

- **Automation and Technological Change:** The impact of new technologies on employment, worker alienation, and capitalist production.

- **Environmental Crisis:** The capitalist drive for profit contributing to environmental degradation and climate change.
