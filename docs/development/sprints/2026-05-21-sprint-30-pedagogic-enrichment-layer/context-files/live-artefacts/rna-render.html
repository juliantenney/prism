Self-Study Session: RNA Viruses and Hepatitis C (HCV)

Audience: Undergraduate students studying virology or molecular biology

Introduction and Study Orientation

This self-directed study page is designed to deepen your understanding of RNA viruses, with a special focus on Hepatitis C virus (HCV). The activities are structured to build your knowledge step-by-step, starting from fundamental differences between RNA virus genome types, then exploring HCV’s unique features and replication mechanisms, and finally clarifying common misconceptions about HCV compared to other RNA viruses like influenza and retroviruses. You will engage with four individual activities that include reading reference materials, completing comparison and summary tables, and answering guided questions. This session is expected to take approximately one hour to complete. Each activity includes prompts to help you reflect on and explain key concepts in your own words, reinforcing your learning and retention.

Reference Text

RNA Virus Genome Types: Positive-Sense vs Negative-Sense RNA

RNA viruses carry their genetic information as RNA genomes. These genomes can be classified based on their polarity:

  • Positive-sense RNA (+RNA):
  • The viral RNA genome has the same polarity as messenger RNA (mRNA).
  • It can be directly recognized by host ribosomes and translated into viral proteins immediately upon infection.
  • No need for a viral polymerase to generate a complementary strand before translation.
  • Example viruses: Hepatitis C virus (HCV), Poliovirus, West Nile virus.
Negative-sense RNA (−RNA):
  • The viral RNA genome is complementary to mRNA and cannot be translated directly.
  • The virus must carry its own RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (virion polymerase) within the virion to synthesize a positive-sense RNA strand from the negative-sense genome.
  • Only the synthesized positive-sense RNA can then be translated by host ribosomes.
  • Example viruses: Influenza virus, Rabies virus, Ebola virus.

Summary of key differences: | Feature | Positive-Sense RNA Virus | Negative-Sense RNA Virus | |--------------------------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------------| | Genome polarity | Same as mRNA | Complementary to mRNA | | Translation | Directly translated by host ribosomes | Requires synthesis of complementary +RNA first | | Viral polymerase | Not packaged in virion (usually) | Packaged in virion (essential) | | Example viruses | Hepatitis C virus, Poliovirus | Influenza virus, Rabies virus |

Hcv Overview Extract

Overview of Hepatitis C Virus (HCV)

Hepatitis C virus (HCV) is a member of the Flaviviridae family and is classified as a positive-sense single-stranded RNA virus. It primarily infects liver cells, known as hepatocytes, where it replicates its genome and produces new virus particles.

A unique feature of HCV replication is its interaction with a host cellular factor called microRNA-122 (miR-122). This small RNA molecule binds to the 5' untranslated region (5' UTR) of the HCV RNA genome. The binding of miR-122 stabilizes the viral RNA, protecting it from degradation and enhancing viral replication efficiency.

Key points:

  • HCV is a positive-sense flavivirus.
  • Replication occurs in hepatocytes.
  • Host microRNA-122 binds to the 5' UTR of HCV RNA, stabilizing the genome and promoting replication.

Ns5b Description

The NS5B Protein of Hepatitis C Virus

NS5B is a viral protein encoded by the Hepatitis C virus genome. It functions as an RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp), an enzyme that synthesizes RNA from an RNA template. This enzyme is essential for the replication of the HCV genome.

Because HCV has an RNA genome, it cannot use the host cell’s DNA-dependent RNA polymerases. Instead, NS5B catalyzes the synthesis of new viral RNA strands by copying the positive-sense RNA genome into a complementary negative-sense RNA intermediate and then back into positive-sense RNA genomes for packaging into new virions.

Without NS5B, the virus cannot replicate its RNA genome, making this protein a critical target for antiviral drugs.

Misconceptions Briefing

Clarifying Common Misconceptions About HCV, Influenza, and Retroviruses
  • Influenza virus has a segmented negative-sense RNA genome. This means its genome is divided into multiple separate RNA segments. This segmentation allows for reassortment (mixing of segments) during co-infection, contributing to influenza’s genetic diversity.
  • Retroviruses (e.g., HIV) carry RNA genomes but replicate through a DNA intermediate. They reverse transcribe their RNA into DNA and integrate this DNA into the host cell genome. This integration is a hallmark of retroviral replication.
  • Hepatitis C virus (HCV) differs from both:
  • It has a non-segmented, positive-sense RNA genome.
  • It does not integrate into the host DNA. Instead, it replicates entirely in the cytoplasm using its RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (NS5B).
  • Therefore, HCV replication and genome organization are distinct from influenza’s segmented genome and retroviral integration.

Misconceptions often arise because:

  • Both influenza and HCV are RNA viruses, but only influenza has a segmented genome.
  • Because retroviruses have RNA genomes and integrate into host DNA, some mistakenly believe HCV also integrates, which it does not.
